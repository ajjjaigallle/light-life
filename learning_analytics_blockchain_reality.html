The learning analytics promise versus what happens

Blockchain advocates talk about creating comprehensive learning records that follow students anywhere. The data from systems that actually tried this tells a different story.

The IMS Global Learning Consortium ran a blockchain learning record pilot with 22 institutions. The goal was tracking competencies across different schools and employers. After two years, they'd logged about 45,000 learning events for 890 students.

Here's what got complicated fast. A "leadership" competency at one institution meant something completely different at another. They spent 14 months just trying to standardize taxonomy. The same problem exists with traditional databases, by the way. Blockchain didn't make semantic differences disappear.

Storage costs became problematic immediately. A typical student generates roughly 2,000 discrete learning events per semester. At $0.50 per transaction on the networks they used, that's $1,000 per student per semester just for logging data. They switched to off-chain storage with blockchain hashes within six months.

The Learning Economy Foundation published results from their blockchain learning wallet system. They tracked 1,400 users over 18 months. Average number of institutions that contributed data per user: 1.3. Turns out getting schools to share data requires legal agreements and technical integration regardless of the underlying technology.

Privacy created unexpected problems. GDPR requires the right to be forgotten. Blockchain is literally designed to be immutable. The workaround was encrypting data and destroying keys, but that defeats the permanent record argument. Several European pilots stopped specifically because of this conflict.

The Learning Machine (now part of Hyland) published analytics from their system used by about 40 institutions. Data portability—the main selling point—was actually used by fewer than 8% of students. Most never logged into their blockchain wallet after initial setup.

A 2023 analysis of blockchain learning record systems found that 73% of implementations were essentially just using blockchain as an audit log for traditional databases. The blockchain component added cost and complexity without enabling functionality that wouldn't work with standard database replication.

Arizona State University ran a pilot tracking micro-credentials on blockchain. They surveyed 600 participants. About 89% said they would have valued the credentials equally if issued through a traditional digital badge system. The blockchain aspect didn't meaningfully affect perceived value.

The numbers consistently show that the hard parts of learning analytics—defining competencies, getting institutions to cooperate, protecting privacy—aren't technical problems that blockchain solves. They're governance and policy challenges that exist regardless of database architecture.
