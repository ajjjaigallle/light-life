What happened when schools actually tried this

About 200 blockchain education pilots have launched since 2016. Most quietly disappeared. The ones that published results are worth examining.

The University of Melbourne ran a pilot issuing 3,000 blockchain credentials in 2018-2019. They tracked how many graduates actually used them: 127 people, or 4.2%. Most graduates stuck with PDF copies because they were easier to attach to job applications. The university didn't renew their blockchain contract.

Sony Global Education tested a blockchain transcript system across 15 institutions in Japan. Implementation took 18 months instead of the projected 6 months. The main bottleneck wasn't technology—it was getting registrars to agree on standardized data formats. Something that would have been an issue with any shared database system.

Malta launched a nationwide blockchain credential program in 2019, budgeting €230,000. By 2022, they'd issued about 5,600 certificates to a student population of roughly 36,000. Adoption rate of 15.5%, which sounds decent until you realize the traditional system still had to run in parallel because not everyone participated.

The Blockcerts consortium published data from 43 participating institutions. Average cost per credential dropped from $12 to $8 over three years as they optimized. Still 4-16 times more expensive than traditional digital credentials. The report didn't track employer adoption metrics.

Southern New Hampshire University issued blockchain badges for competency-based programs. They surveyed 400 recipients six months later. About 22% had shared them publicly. Interestingly, 68% still preferred traditional transcripts when applying to graduate programs because admissions offices didn't know what to do with blockchain credentials.

The European Commission's EBSI project across 30 universities tracked verification attempts. Over 18 months, they recorded 892 verification requests for roughly 40,000 issued credentials. That's a 2.2% verification rate. Traditional credentials see verification rates around 8-12%.

Open University in the UK published honest numbers: their blockchain pilot cost £180,000 and issued 1,200 credentials. Traditional system cost for the same number: approximately £2,400. They cited "learning experience" as the primary value, which is basically admitting it wasn't cost-effective.

The pattern across these pilots shows the same issues. Implementation takes longer than expected, costs more than traditional systems, and actual usage remains low. The technology works, but none of these pilots demonstrated clear advantages that justified the resource investment.
