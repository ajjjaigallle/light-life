Making Blockchain Real

Not theoretical. Not abstract. Just practical skills.

Since 2016, we've been teaching blockchain through actual implementation. Our workshops focus on what you can build, not what you might understand someday. Lighter DEX exists because education shouldn't stop at borders.

How We Got Here

Back in 2016, blockchain education was mostly whitepapers and theoretical discussions. We started because we needed something different. Something that actually taught you how to deploy a smart contract, how to debug a consensus issue, how to architect a distributed application that doesn't fall apart under load.

The first workshop had eleven participants spread across four countries. We experimented with lighter dex implementations, walked through actual code, and fixed real problems together. That hands-on approach stuck. No PowerPoint presentations about revolutionary potential. Just terminals open, code running, problems getting solved.

Now we're teaching hundreds of students each year, but the philosophy hasn't changed. Start with a problem, work through the solution, understand what broke and why. Blockchain technology stops being mysterious when you've spent three hours troubleshooting gas optimization or implementing merkle tree verification yourself.

Who Builds This

Our instructors come from actual blockchain projects. They've dealt with the production issues, optimization challenges, and architectural decisions that textbooks skip over.

Arvid Lindström

Lead Blockchain Educator

Arvid spent six years building distributed systems before joining us. He's worked on proof-of-stake implementations, designed cross-chain bridges, and debugged more consensus failures than anyone should have to. His workshops focus on the unglamorous parts that actually matter: how to handle network partitions, why your gas estimates are wrong, what happens when validators disagree. He teaches the way he learned—by breaking things until you understand them.

What Drives Us

  • Accessibility shouldn't depend on geography. A student in Limerick and one in Lagos should have the same shot at understanding distributed ledgers.
  • Practice beats theory every time. You remember the smart contract you debugged for three hours, not the lecture about EVM architecture.
  • Real problems teach better than perfect examples. We use messy datasets, edge cases, and scenarios that don't have clean solutions.
  • Learning works better when you're building something actual. Our assignments produce functioning code, not completed worksheets.
  • Quality education shouldn't require expensive in-person programs. Online doesn't mean inferior if you design it properly.

Let's Talk Blockchain
