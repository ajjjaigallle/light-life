Running the numbers on what this actually uses

Nobody talks about this much, but blockchain education systems use electricity. The numbers are worth running.

Bitcoin and Ethereum combined consume roughly 200 terawatt-hours annually. For reference, that's about the same as Thailand's entire energy consumption. Education applications represent a tiny fraction of blockchain activity, but the per-transaction costs still apply.

Before Ethereum switched to proof-of-stake in September 2022, a single transaction consumed about 238 kWh of electricity. Issuing a credential required 2-3 transactions. So one blockchain diploma used roughly 600 kWh, equivalent to powering an average U.S. home for 20 days. The carbon footprint was approximately 300 kg of CO2.

Post-merge Ethereum dropped energy use by about 99.95%, which sounds incredible. Current per-transaction energy consumption sits around 0.01 kWh. That's comparable to sending a few emails. Much more reasonable, but still about 20 times more energy than writing to a traditional database.

Here's where scale matters. MIT's Digital Credentials Consortium has issued roughly 30,000 blockchain credentials. At current Ethereum energy rates, that's about 300 kWh total, or $45 worth of electricity. Not terrible. But if every degree worldwide went on blockchain, we're talking about 2.2 million kWh annually, even with efficient proof-of-stake systems.

Alternative blockchains market themselves as more efficient. Algorand claims 0.0004 kWh per transaction. Cardano reports similar numbers. But adoption is sparse. About 94% of education blockchain projects still use either Bitcoin or Ethereum according to a 2023 survey of implementations.

The comparison with traditional systems is straightforward. AWS estimates that storing and serving a 50KB digital credential uses approximately 0.0005 kWh over its lifetime. That's 20 times less than even the most efficient blockchain options.

Environmental costs extend beyond energy. Blockchain nodes require computing hardware. A full Ethereum node needs about 2TB of storage and runs continuously. Traditional credential databases can run on shared infrastructure. The University of Nicosia reported running four dedicated blockchain nodes at 350W each, 24/7. That's 30,000 kWh annually just for infrastructure, before any transactions.

The counterargument is that blockchain enables decentralization, which provides benefits worth the energy cost. Maybe. But the data shows most education blockchains are somewhat centralized anyway, with credentials issued by a limited number of authorized nodes. You're getting centralized system inefficiency with distributed system energy costs.

The environmental numbers aren't catastrophic, especially post-merge. But they're measurably worse than traditional alternatives for a use case where the benefits remain unclear.
