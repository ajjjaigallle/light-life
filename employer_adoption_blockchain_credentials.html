Who actually checks these things

The blockchain credential pitch assumes employers will verify them. The data on employer adoption is pretty sparse.

A 2023 survey by the National Association of Colleges and Employers contacted 2,100 HR professionals. They asked about blockchain credential awareness: 18% had heard of it, 4% understood how it worked, and 0.8% had actually verified a blockchain credential.

The companies that do accept blockchain credentials are mostly tech firms that issue them to their own employees. IBM, Microsoft, and SAP represent about 60% of all blockchain credential verifications according to Credly's published data. That's not really broad adoption—it's early adopters verifying each other.

Here's a practical problem. To verify a blockchain credential, you need compatible software. Most applicant tracking systems don't integrate with blockchain verification. So even interested employers end up asking for traditional transcripts anyway because their systems can't process the blockchain version.

The Society for Human Resource Management surveyed 800 members about credential verification practices. About 89% still use either the National Student Clearinghouse or direct phone calls to registrars. Only 3% had any awareness that blockchain credentials existed as an option.

LinkedIn, which processes millions of job applications, added blockchain credential support in 2018. They published usage statistics in 2022: fewer than 12,000 profiles included blockchain credentials. For context, LinkedIn has about 900 million users. That's 0.001% adoption.

Small and medium businesses present another challenge. They typically don't verify credentials at all or use basic background check services. These services haven't integrated blockchain verification because there's no demand. The market is circular—employers don't adopt because few credentials exist, and few credentials exist because employers don't adopt.

International hiring complicates things further. A blockchain credential from a Chinese university needs to be understood by an American employer. But the trust issue isn't about verification technology—it's about whether the employer recognizes the institution as legitimate. Blockchain doesn't solve credential evaluation across different education systems.

The companies actually building blockchain credential systems acknowledge this. Velocity Network Foundation's 2023 report noted that "employer adoption remains the primary barrier to scaling." They've partnered with about 80 employers globally. Sounds decent until you realize there are roughly 300 million businesses worldwide.

Healthcare shows what happens in regulated industries. Medical facilities must verify credentials, and they're quick to adopt useful technology. Yet only 2% use any blockchain-based verification according to a survey of 400 medical HR departments. They stick with traditional verification because it works, it's cheaper, and their auditors understand it.
